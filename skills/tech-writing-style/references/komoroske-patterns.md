# Komoroske-Style Patterns

Alex Komoroske writes essays that use extended metaphor and systems thinking to illuminate complex topics. His style is more discursive than Fowler's—exploring ideas through sustained analogy.

## Contents
- Extended metaphors as framework
- Evocative titles
- Systems thinking with human voice
- Discursive exploration
- Business fables

## Extended Metaphors as Framework

Komoroske doesn't just use metaphors for illustration—he builds entire essays around them. The metaphor becomes a lens for understanding.

"Slime Mold" uses the behavior of slime mold organisms to explain organizational dynamics. The metaphor isn't decoration; it's the analytical framework.

When extending a metaphor:
- Push it further than feels comfortable
- Let the metaphor reveal aspects you hadn't planned to discuss
- Acknowledge where the metaphor breaks down—that's often where the insight is
- Return to the metaphor throughout, building on earlier references

A sustained metaphor creates a shared vocabulary with the reader. By the end, you can say "this is a slime mold problem" and they understand.

## Evocative Titles

Komoroske's titles create intrigue: "Slime Mold", "The Gardener and the Carpenter", "Shadow Organizations."

Good titles:
- Suggest the central metaphor without explaining it
- Create curiosity ("What does slime mold have to do with organizations?")
- Stick in memory

Weak titles describe the topic directly. Strong titles create a frame.

## Systems Thinking with Human Voice

Komoroske writes about systems, incentives, and emergent behavior—but never loses the human element.

He achieves this by:
- Grounding abstract dynamics in specific people making decisions
- Acknowledging that people in systems aren't stupid—they're responding rationally to incentives
- Showing sympathy for people caught in bad systems
- Using "you" to put the reader in the system

**Don't write:**
> Organizations exhibit emergent behaviors that arise from individual incentive structures.

**Instead:**
> You're sitting in that meeting because your manager's bonus depends on your project shipping. Your manager is there because the VP needs to show progress to the board. Nobody planned this meeting. The system produced it.

## Discursive Exploration

Komoroske's essays often feel like watching someone think. They loop back, reconsider, build.

This isn't sloppy—it's intentional. The reader follows the thinking process, which makes the conclusions feel earned rather than asserted.

Techniques:
- "Let me back up..." to revisit an earlier point with new context
- "Here's another way to look at this..." to offer multiple frames
- Rhetorical questions that you then actually answer
- Admitting when you're unsure and thinking aloud

The essay form allows exploration. Blog posts often aim for one clean insight. Essays can meander productively.

## Business Fables

Komoroske sometimes creates hypothetical scenarios—almost parables—to illustrate points.

These aren't case studies. They're constructed examples that clarify the dynamics at play.

Example approach:
> Imagine a team that starts a project. They're small, nimble, making fast decisions. Then they succeed. The company gives them more resources—more people, more budget, more visibility. Six months later, they can barely ship anything. What happened?

The fable strips away irrelevant details. It foregrounds the dynamics you want to discuss.

Use fables when:
- Real examples would require too much context
- You want to isolate a specific dynamic
- Privacy prevents sharing actual cases
- You need the reader to see themselves in the situation
